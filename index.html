Case Quality — The Core Change
The SML CI replaces the generic PI quality matrix with a dedicated five-dimension med mal matrix
Without SML custom instructions, every call is scored against the base prompt's Personal Injury Case Quality Matrix — a broad rubric built for car accidents and general tort claims. That matrix has no concept of clinical negligence type, causation clarity in a medical context, or SOL rules specific to the medical discovery rule.

The SML CI installs a Medical Malpractice Quality Matrix that evaluates each case across five dimensions and a modifier stack. This is why the same facts can produce a completely different grade depending on which instructions are active — the model is running a different calculation entirely.
D1 — Malpractice Type
1–5 from general dissatisfaction to birth injury / wrongful death / gross negligence. Forces classification of the specific clinical failure, not just "something went wrong."
D2 — Harm Severity
1–5 from no measurable harm to catastrophic or fatal. Maps directly to SML's Red Flag Injury list — death, permanent neurologic injury, ICU admission following routine care.
D3 — Causation Clarity
1–5 from caller speculating to confirmed deviation (written admission, retained expert, or obvious error). This dimension is what makes SML's discovery questions critical — agents who ask the right questions move this score up.
D4 — Damages Potential
1–5 from minor pain and suffering to wrongful death with dependents / permanent disability. Grounds the grade in economic reality, not just clinical severity.
D5 — SOL Viability
1–5 from clearly time-barred to incident within 12 months. Explicitly accounts for the medical discovery rule — SOL can run from date of discovery, not incident date.
Modifiers
+2 wrongful death · +2 birth injury · +1 written confirmation · +1 prior pattern · −1 pre-existing condition · −1 no records · −2 sovereign immunity · −2 government provider
Grade thresholds: A = 22–27 (refer immediately) · B = 17–21 (solid referral) · C = 12–16 (marginal) · D = 5–11 (weak or non-viable). Death and birth injury cases can never be graded below B regardless of dimension scores.

Coaching Quality — The Most Significant Qualitative Shift
From outcome-based critique to case-quality-driven, protocol-specific instruction
Scores and grades measure what happened. Coaching determines whether agents learn the right lesson. The SML CI changed the coaching logic in four ways that carry across all six calls.
1
Case grade now drives coaching emphasis. For Grade B cases (Nathan Kaplan, Craig Santoro), coaching focuses on referral protocol and escalation failures — the case was viable and high-value, the process broke down. For Grade C cases (Jeffrey, Stacey), coaching focuses on information-gathering gaps — the case might be viable but the agent didn't develop enough facts to know. For Grade D (Kenneth Bucknam), coaching focuses on claim triage — how to separate viable embedded claims from non-viable ones. The grade tells the agent what they're dealing with; the coaching tells them what they did wrong relative to that specific situation.
2
Discovery questions are the coaching anchor for C-grade cases. Every agent who declined or terminated a Grade C call without asking SML's required Section 2 questions is coached on exactly which questions were skipped and why they matter for CausationClarity — not "gather more information before deciding" but "you needed to ask about compensable harm, provider identity, and negligence type. Answers to those questions could have moved D3 from 2 to 3+, which may have changed the referral path."
3
Auto-Escalate coaching is trigger-specific, not generic. The base prompt tells agents to escalate sooner. The SML CI names the exact clinical indicator that triggered the rule: "Post-operative sepsis following elective surgery is a Red Flag Injury under SML Section 2. Immediate routing to an attorney or medical reviewer is required — not a records-hold, not a scheduled callback, not an email review." The trigger is named. The required action is named. The section is cited. Agents know precisely what to look for next time.
4
Correct behavior is now explicitly credited. SML's no-penalty scenarios introduce affirmative reinforcement that the base prompt's deficiency-only coaching doesn't provide. Agents who applied correct protocol — Kristian's SOL warning, Jeffrey's mid-call re-engagement, Andrea's empathy on a sensitive call — receive coaching notes that say so explicitly. This matters for training: agents need to know what to repeat, not just what to avoid.
